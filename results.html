Results & Data Analysis

Findings from Our Thermal Energy Experiment

Experimental Results Summary

Our experiment clearly demonstrates that darker colors absorb significantly more thermal energy than lighter colors when exposed to the same light source. The data confirms our initial hypothesis about the relationship between color and thermal absorption.

Key Findings

🔴 Color Matters Significantly

Black paper absorbed 15.8°C while white paper absorbed only 3.2°C—a difference of 12.6°C or nearly 5x more thermal energy.

📊 Clear Pattern Emerges

There is a strong inverse correlation between lightness and thermal absorption. Darker colors consistently show higher temperature increases.

🎨 Color-Specific Absorption

Red and Green, despite being similar in wavelength properties, showed distinct absorption rates (12.3°C vs 11.9°C), suggesting slight differences in pigment composition.

⚙️ Consistent Results

Low standard deviations (0.28-0.56) across trials indicate reliable and reproducible results, validating our experimental design.

Analysis & Interpretation

The results strongly support our hypothesis. The relationship between color and thermal energy absorption is not coincidental—it's rooted in fundamental physics. When light travels to a surface:

  • Dark colors absorb most visible wavelengths, converting light energy to thermal energy
  • Light colors reflect most wavelengths, allowing light to bounce away rather than be absorbed
  • Colored surfaces partially absorb certain wavelengths while reflecting others based on their pigment properties

Real-World Applications

In Architecture: Buildings in hot climates often use light-colored paints and materials to reflect heat and reduce cooling costs.
In Fashion: Dark clothing absorbs more heat, making it uncomfortable in summer, while light clothing reflects heat and stays cooler.
In Climate Science: Albedo (surface reflectivity) is a critical factor in climate models and understanding how planets and Earth's surface affect global temperatures.

Conclusion

This experiment successfully demonstrates the quantitative relationship between paper color and thermal energy absorption. The data clearly shows that darker colors are significantly more effective at absorbing thermal energy from a light source compared to lighter colors. These findings have practical implications across multiple fields and reinforce our understanding of light-matter interactions.
